Vienna Game

The Vienna starts with the moves 1.e4 e5 2.Nc3. (seen above)

It is arguably a counterpart to the King's Knight Opening. White chooses to play a little passively in their second move.

Falkbeer Variation: 2...Nf6

Nf6 is probably the more unpopular move after Nc3, but it is still completely playable and not risky at all.

Vienna Gambit: 3.f4

The Vienna Gambit at master level is too risky to play, but for any opponent below (2000), it is worth a try.

3...exf4

White's idea with the gambit is to invite 3...exf4, which is considered bad for black since white can just push their pawn to 4.e5, kicking black's knight out and forcing them to go 4...Ng8. This retreat is obviously bad for black (losing about 1 eval rating), white will gain an advantage in development as well as center quality.

After black goes back to 4...Ng8, white immediately has to go 5.Nf3, (other moves are also playable, this is the best and the most intuitive though) because if they don't, they will (after e.g. 5.d4) be met with a devastating 5...Qh4+, which will either completely destroy white's king side (6.g3 fxg3 7.hxg3 Qxh1), or make white lose the ability to castle (6.Ke2 or 6.Kd2).

5.Nf3 is quite frequently met with 5...d6, where white's best response is 6.d4, solidifying their center. When black plays 6...dxe5, white needs to be careful not to take the pawn back instantly, because 7.dxe5 is met with 7...Qxd1+ 8.Kxd1, losing the right to castle, and 7.Nxe5 leads to the same devastation as before, after 7...Qh4+. Instead, white needs to wait just one move, playing 7.Qe2. After e.g. 7...Be7, white can finally play 8.Qxe5.

3...Nc6

After black declines the gambit and instead chooses to develop their knight, white still has a sneaky attack coming, achieving the same result. To force black's knight back to g8 again, white first plays fxe5. When black plays Nxe5, white pushes their pawn to d4, and after Nc6 (or Ng6) e5, black is forced to go Ng8 once more.

3...d5

This is black's best response, where they have an incredibly slight advantage. This move is very difficult to find by yourself and almost no one will play it who hasn't studied the Vienna Gambit. After 4.fxe5 4...Nxe4, white's best move is 5.Qf3. Black has three logical moves here. The best move, and also least common of the three is 5...f5 (Bardeleben Variation). White should play 6.d3 here, kicking the knight away. Because nothing else wins for black, they have to play 6...Nxc3 7.bxc3. The second best move is 5...Nc6. White should just play 6.Bb5, and let black take the knight. The most common move, 5...Nxc3, needs to be met with 6.bxc3, because if white plays 6.dxc4, well, they lose a central pawn, and 6.Qxc3 doesn't work because of either 6...d4, where white's queen is in the middle of the board where it will be harassed, or 6...Qh4+, whereafter 7.Qg3 is best, because after 7.g3, black has a devastating 7...Qe4+, forking the king and the rook on h1. (8.Kf2 9...Qxh1)

Stanley Variation: 3.Bc4

This variation can create some equal positions, or some great imbalances.

Frankeinstein-Dracula Variation: 3...Nxe4

The idea of this is simple. Sacrificing a knight temporarily, then winning it back. After 4.Nxe4, white is losing by a bit because black has 4...d5, forking the bishop and knight. White's best move is 5.Bd3, and when met with 5...dxe4, just take back with 6.Bxe4. Instead, white should go 4.Qh5 (hoping for [4...Nxc3??] 5.Qxf7#) 4...Nd6, defending the checkmate, and counterattacking the bishop on c4. The game usually continues with 5.Bb5 Be7, with black kicking away white's queen, while white tries to develop their pieces.

3...Bc5

This move can transpose to a Giuoco Piano Game very easily. This variation doesn't create any imbalances and most likely leads to an equal position.

White can potentially transpose into a King's Gambit Declined after 4.d3 d6 5.f4 Nf6 6.Nf3.

Mieses Variation: 3.g3

This is a more quiet approach to the Falkbeer, with white fianchettoing their king's bishop.

The main line is considered to be 3...d5 4.exd5 Nxd5 5.Bg2 (White can not play 5.Nxd5 because of 5...Qxd5, where black gains a massive center advantage) 5...Nxc3 6.Bxc3 Bd6 7.Nf3 O-O 8.O-O.

A major sideline for black is 3...Bc5 4. Bg2. (Note that 3...Nc6 transposes into another line [Max Lange, Paulsen Variation])

Mengarini Variation: 3.a3

This is an interesting response by white, it does not create an attack, nor does it develop a piece. It is essentially a waiting move, which creates lots of "reversed" variations of King's Pawn: 1...e5 openings.

3...d5 gives white an improved version of the Scotch Game after 4.exd5 Nxd5 5.Qh5. Black is not advised to play 5...Nxc3, because they lose a pawn after 6.Qxe5+ Be7 7.Qxc3, instead, the best move is 5...Nc6.

3...Bc5 4.Nf3 gives black a reversed Two Knights Defence.

Max Lange Defense: 2...Nc6

This is the more common variation of the Vienna. Note that 3.Nf3 transposes the game into the Three Knights Opening.

Vienna Gambit: 3.f4

This gambit aims to sacrifice a pawn for more control of the center. After this, black is instantly winning with an advantage of about -0.8. I personally do not reccommend this, but you totally play it if you keel like it.

This turns into the Hamppe-Muzio Gambit after 3...exf4 4.Nf3 g5 5.Bc4 g4 6.O-O which has a Dubois Variation represented by the moves 6...gxf3 7.Qxf3 Ne5 8.Qxf4 Qf6, and the Pierce Gambit after 6.d4

This is called the Steinitz Gambit after 3...exf4 4.d4 (Black is now winning even more)

Paulsen Variation: 3.g3

This variation aims to play more quietly, and fianchetto white's bishop.

3...Nf6

This is the most common response to the Paulsen, black just develops their knight.

The most intuitive move, as well as the best one is 4.Bg2, to which black mostly plays 4...Bc5. This transposes the opening into a Falkbeer: Mieses.

White has a move which is equally as good, 4.Nf3. To this, black has three good responses. Both 4...Bc5 and 4...Bb4 result in 5.Bg2. In the former variation, life goes on normally and we just get a happy four knights with a fianchetto. In the ladder one, however black can play 5...Bxc3, and is advised to do so. In this case, white just plays 6.bxc3.

3...Bc5

An also prominent response.

The best move here is 4.Bg2, fianchettoing white's king side bishop. Black here either has 4...Nf6, which, once again, transposes into Falkbeer: Mieses.

Another great response is 4...d6, whereafter white either has 5.Na4, trading black's bishop for the knight, 5.d3, or 5.Nge2.
